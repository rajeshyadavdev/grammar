Subject–Verb Agreement

Avoid common mistakes by learning how subjects and verbs work together perfectly.

The Rules of Agreement

Rule 1: Singular vs. Plural

The basic rule is simple: A singular subject needs a singular verb, and a plural subject needs a plural verb.

• Singular: The cat sleeps. (Add 's' to the verb)
• Plural: The cats sleep. (No 's' on the verb)

Remember: In the present tense, if the subject is He, She, or It, the verb almost always ends in -s or -es.

Rule 2: Using "And" (Compound Subjects)

When you join two subjects with "and", they become plural. You must use a plural verb.

• "Sarah and Tom are late." (Not 'is')
• "The dog and the cat run in the park." (Not 'runs')

Pro-Tip: Think of "and" as a plus sign ($1 + 1 = 2$), making the subject plural.

Rule 3: Everyone, Someone, & Nobody

These words might sound like they talk about many people, but in grammar, they are always singular.

• ❌ "Everyone are happy."
• ✅ "Everyone is happy."
• ❌ "Somebody know the answer."
• ✅ "Somebody knows the answer."

Rule 4: Words in Between

Don't be fooled by words that come between the subject and the verb! Focus only on the main subject.

• "The box of chocolates is empty."
(The subject is 'box', not 'chocolates')

• "The students in the hallway were loud."
(The subject is 'students', not 'hallway')

Rule 5: Groups (Collective Nouns)

Words like Team, Family, Group, and Class are usually treated as a single unit (Singular).

• "My family lives in New York."
• "The team plays well today."

Quick Correction Guide

Subject Type Incorrect Correct
He / She / It He walk home. He walks home.
They / We They is tired. They are tired.
Uncountable The water are cold. The water is cold.
Questions Do she like tea? Does she like tea?
