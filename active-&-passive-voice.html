Active & Passive Voice Explained

Master the art of shifting focus in your sentences. Learn when to highlight the 'doer' and when to highlight the 'result.'

From Beginner to Advanced

Level 1: What is the Difference?

In Active Voice, the subject performs the action. It is direct and strong.
• "The chef (S) cooks (V) the meal (O)."

In Passive Voice, the subject receives the action. The focus moves to the result.
• "The meal (S) is cooked (V) by the chef."

When to use Active: Use it for most writing to be clear and energetic.

Level 2: How to Form the Passive Voice

To make a passive sentence, you need two special ingredients:

1. The Verb "To Be": (am, is, are, was, were, been).
2. Past Participle (V3): (eaten, written, broken, cleaned).

Formula: Object + To Be + V3 + (by Subject).
• Active: "I wrote the letter."
• Passive: "The letter was written by me."

Level 3: When to use Passive (Advanced)

Advanced writers use Passive Voice for specific reasons:

• The 'Doer' is unknown: "The bank was robbed." (We don't know who did it).
• The result is more important: "A cure for cancer was discovered."
• Formal/Scientific Writing: "The chemicals were mixed in the lab."

Level 4: Changing Tenses

The verb "To Be" changes based on the time of the action:

• Present: "The house is cleaned daily."
• Past: "The house was cleaned yesterday."
• Future: "The house will be cleaned tomorrow."
• Continuous: "The house is being cleaned right now."

Quick Transformation Chart

Tense Active Voice Passive Voice
Simple Present He opens the door. The door is opened.
Simple Past He opened the door. The door was opened.
Present Continuous He is opening the door. The door is being opened.
Present Perfect He has opened the door. The door has been opened.
Modals He must open the door. The door must be opened.
